How to choose the right smart body scale

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A smart body scale is worth buying if you want more than just weight, it can estimate body fat, muscle mass, and other metrics by sending a tiny electrical current through your body. Focus on scales that connect to apps you already use (like Apple Health or Fitbit) and support unlimited users if you share the scale. Accuracy for body fat is decent for tracking trends, but don’t treat the numbers as clinical-grade data.

How do smart scales measure body composition?

Most smart scales use a technology called bioelectrical impedance analysis (BIA). Tiny electrodes on the scale’s surface send a low-level electrical current up through your bare feet as you stand on it. The current travels faster through lean tissue (which has more water) than through fat tissue, and the scale uses that resistance along with your height, age, and gender to estimate things like body fat percentage, muscle mass, and bone mass. It’s a non-invasive process that takes only a few seconds, but keep in mind that BIA is sensitive to hydration levels and recent meals. For consistent trends, weigh yourself at the same time each morning before eating or drinking. The technology is well-established and reliable enough for home use, but it’s not a substitute for a DEXA scan if you need medical precision.

What metrics should you actually care about?

The headline numbers, weight and body fat percentage, are what most people track, and they’re usually enough for general fitness goals. Many scales also display muscle mass, bone mass, visceral fat rating, and water percentage. Some add a “metabolic age” or “body age” estimate, which can be motivational but is largely a derived figure based on the other readings. For practical purposes, focus on weight, body fat percentage, and maybe muscle mass if you’re strength training. Visceral fat can be a useful health indicator, but remember that no home scale can measure it directly. The extra metrics (like bone mass or protein level) tend to be rough estimates and shouldn’t be taken as clinical values. If a scale overwhelms with too many numbers, look for one that lets you customize which readings appear on the app dashboard.

Does the scale sync with your existing health apps?

Apple Health, Google Fit, Fitbit, Samsung Health, and MyFitnessPal are the most common app ecosystems. Most major smart scales sync automatically via Bluetooth or Wi-Fi to their own companion app, which then pushes data to these platforms. Check the scale’s compatibility list before buying; some scales work with Apple Health but not Google Fit, or vice versa. Wi-Fi sync is convenient because the scale uploads readings automatically without needing your phone nearby, but Bluetooth-only scales require your phone to be within range. If you use multiple health apps, confirm that the scale can push data to all of them simultaneously. A few scales also support direct integration with a smartwatch or fitness tracker, which can streamline your routine.

How do multi-user scales recognize different people?

Most multi-user scales use weight as the primary identifying factor. When you step on, the scale compares your weight against previously stored user profiles and assigns the reading to the closest match. This works well if household members have noticeably different body weights, but can get confused if two people are within a few pounds of each other. Better scales supplement weight detection with additional cues like impedance patterns or require you to select your profile on the app before stepping on. Many support unlimited user profiles, which is ideal for families or roommates. A few scales also let you connect multiple fitness apps to the same scale, so each person’s data goes to their own app account.

How accurate are smart scales really?

For weight, smart scales are generally very accurate, usually within a fraction of a pound of a calibrated mechanical scale. For body composition metrics, accuracy is more variable. The same scale can give consistent readings day to day (good for tracking trends), but the absolute numbers may differ from what you’d get from a medical-grade device. User reviews and expert roundups consistently note that factors like hydration, time of day, and skin moisture can sway the readings by a few percentage points for body fat. That’s fine for seeing whether your body composition is improving over months, but don’t fixate on a single day’s number. Scales that offer “athlete mode” or advanced algorithms tend to be slightly more reliable for people with very low or very high body fat levels.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need Wi-Fi or is Bluetooth enough?

Bluetooth is fine if you always keep your phone nearby when you weigh in and don’t mind opening the app to sync. Wi-Fi is more convenient for households where the scale sits in a different room than the phone, and it allows data to upload automatically without any extra steps.

Can I use a smart scale without a smartphone?

Most smart scales require a smartphone to set them up and view detailed metrics. A few models have a small built-in display that shows the basic numbers and can be used standalone, but you’ll lose the history and trend charts unless you sync occasionally.

How does the scale distinguish between users?

It usually recognizes users by weight or a combination of weight and impedance. For the best experience, choose a scale that also lets you manually select your profile in the app, because weight-based recognition can stumble when household members are similarly sized.

Are smart scales safe during pregnancy?

The electrical current used in BIA is extremely low and considered safe for most people, but many manufacturers advise against using smart scales during pregnancy as a precautionary measure. If you’re pregnant, check the scale’s manual or ask your doctor before regular use.

Can I use a smart scale on carpet?

Smart scales work fine on hard, level surfaces like tile or hardwood. On thick carpet, the scale may not sit evenly and can give inconsistent readings. If you must place it on carpet, look for a scale with longer, sturdier feet or a “carpet mode” in the manual, but a hard surface is always preferred.

What’s the real difference between a ‘smart scale’ and a ‘body composition scale’?

These terms are often used interchangeably. Any scale that measures body fat and other metrics beyond weight is a body composition scale, and “smart” simply means it can connect to an app. So nearly all modern body composition scales are also smart scales.

In shortThe best smart scale for you comes down to which apps you already use, how many people will step on it, and whether you prefer Wi-Fi convenience or a lower upfront investment. Focus on a model that measures the handful of metrics you’ll actually glance at each morning, and remember that consistency in when and how you weigh is more important than the absolute numbers. A smart scale is a practical tool for keeping an eye on your health trends, just don’t let the daily fluctuations distract you from the bigger picture.
